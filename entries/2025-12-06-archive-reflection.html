journal 12/6/25 — reflecting on the archive
I spent some time rereading the entries I’ve dropped into this little digital terrarium of mine, and it’s funny how obvious the connective tissue becomes when I put them side by side. Every single one—whether I’m talking about panic attacks, sleep, therapy dogs, or whatever version of self-experimenting I happened to be doing that morning—circles the same gravitational center: I’m always trying to understand the machinery behind the feeling. It’s not enough for me to feel something; I immediately start narrating the mechanism, the pattern, the system. It’s like I’m treating my own nervous system as a financial audit that just happens to carry my name on the top sheet. There’s a rhythm to these entries. They’re written at the same hours—the thin moments in the morning where the day hasn’t gotten dressed yet—and they all share that quiet tension between exhaustion and clarity. I keep noting sleep, or the lack of it, but it’s rarely written like a complaint. More like an observable data point that might unlock a bigger diagram. Same with anxiety. Same with the stimulants, the little surges, the body stuff. Everything gets cataloged. Everything gets interpreted. And then there’s this other strand—the comforting one—that keeps showing up without me intentionally weaving it. Ruby nosing her way into a panic spell. My wife bringing a soft perimeter around the harder moments. Even the routines I’m trying to build. These entries accidentally reveal how much support I actually have, and how often I forget that in the middle of worrying about the internal circuitry. If I could zoom out and audit the whole set the way I would a balance sheet, I think I’d conclude the following: I’m trying very hard. Almost comically hard sometimes. But there’s sincerity in it. My body keeps raising little flags, and instead of ignoring them, I’m actively decoding them. I’m calmer on the page than I give myself credit for. Even when writing about chaos. The themes repeat because the lessons aren’t learned yet, and that’s fine. This is part of the circuit. I’m starting to see the journal less as “capturing the day” and more as a long-duration study—me monitoring myself with the same obsessive curiosity I bring to work, running, coding, card collecting, all of it. It’s strangely comforting to witness that consistency. Even my neuroses are reliable. If nothing else, reading the older entries reminds me that I’ve already survived every version of the fear I’ve written down. And that’s not a bad thing to carry forward.
