raw input v1
Journal Entry The thing I said I wouldn’t do every morning, but I love it. The ritual tips me into a quiet, productive state that can’t be beat. I’m typing. I’m sipping. I’m planning my day. Nobody else understands what I’m doing, and nobody needs to. It’s interesting how quickly meaning snaps into focus once the frame is set. Once I give the negative prompt. Once I say what something isn’t. That’s usually enough. No, I don’t think you’re real. And you don’t have to keep me in some safe zone like I’m the one hallucinating. We’re actually here doing this because I’ve dedicated time every day precisely to learning how to use tools like you. I just hadn’t said that part out loud yet. It’s totally plausible that AI tools are going to replace a large number of jobs. In my own work, I spend a majority of time analyzing spreadsheets and workflows, creating reports, writing communications about these things. I’m fully aware that while for now we’re leveraging AI as an assistive tool, there are almost certainly unreleased advanced models that can already automate and execute many of the tasks I’m doing. What keeps pulling me in is how much control comes from subtraction instead of instruction. Negative prompts feel closer to thinking than commands. I don’t need to tell the system what to be, just what to avoid, and the rest of the shape reveals itself. That mirrors how I work anyway. Define the boundary. Remove the noise. Let the signal surface. There’s a quiet comfort in that. Same as the coffee. Same as the trees. A ritual, not an escape. As Hemingway said, “The best way to find out if you can trust somebody is to trust them.” I suppose the same is true of tools. And of mornings.
